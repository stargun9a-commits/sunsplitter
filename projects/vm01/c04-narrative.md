### The "Easy AF" Phantom Infrastructure
The documentary beat shifts from the gritty, hardware-locked constraints of the local machine to the sleek, ephemeral nature of the cloud. The protagonist realizes that fighting the host machine for 8GB of VRAM is a losing battle when Google offers 16GB for free.

**Story Beat 1: Severing the Hardware Tether**
We open on the complex, frustrating `launch-drone.sh` script—the VFIO passthrough failing, the NVIDIA drivers clinging to the GPU. The operator hits a wall. The pivot is born not out of luxury, but necessity. The shift to Kaggle is visualized as leaving a heavy, grounded bunker for a lightweight, untraceable cloud glider.

**Story Beat 2: Dialing Out (The Reverse Tunnel)**
Visualizing the VS Code Remote Tunnel. Instead of breaking down Kaggle's firewall doors (which triggers alarms), the script acts like an inside man placing a quiet phone call out. The IDE connects not by attacking, but by answering the call.

**Story Beat 3: The 14-Billion Parameter Brain**
The selection of the Phi-4 model. It's not the biggest brain, but it's the sharpest one that fits in the 16GB box while leaving enough room to "remember" the target enterprise architecture. The visualization of the KV cache expanding as the agent reads through thousands of lines of SAP logs.

**Story Beat 4: Hiding in the Noise**
The Tor `obfs4` bridge is the cloaking device. To Google's servers, the traffic looks like random, chaotic noise. The `ghost_pulse` script beats like a slow heart, keeping the ephemeral container alive just long enough to execute the strike. And when the 12-hour limit hits, the container vanishes, taking all forensic evidence with it into the digital void.
